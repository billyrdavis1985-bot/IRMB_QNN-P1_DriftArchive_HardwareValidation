Turning QPU Drift into a Placement-and-Modeling Instrument
A preregistered pilot linking a month of calibration telemetry, archive-derived noise models, hardware-aware qubit placement, and real IBM quantum execution

Billy R. Davis Jr.
Hudson Forge Technologies · Independent Research in Multi-Agent Benchmarking (IRMB)

Repository: https://github.com/billyrdavis1985-bot/IRMB_QNN-P1_DriftArchive_HardwareValidation

Abstract

Can a noise model built from a self-collected month of quantum-processing-unit calibration data approximate how a quantum neural network will behave on real hardware?

QNN-P1 tested that question through a preregistered three-tier protocol: ideal simulation → archive-seeded noisy simulation → real IBM hardware. The study used hourly ibm_fez calibration snapshots collected by a Raspberry Pi 5, reconstructed first-order Aer noise models from the archive, selected a physically stable eight-qubit chain, and evaluated two trained hybrid quantum models on the real device.

Under the tested conditions, run-day simulation and measured hardware accuracy differed by 0.5 percentage points for both evaluated quantum arms:

Entangled hybrid: 0.7800 simulated, 0.7850 hardware
No-entanglement hybrid: 0.7500 simulated, 0.7550 hardware

The 3.0-point separation between the two arms was reproduced in both simulation and hardware, and all three preregistered endpoints passed against their committed thresholds.

These are two single-run hardware evaluations under one task, seed, backend, physical chain, and calibration window. They support the feasibility of the collector-to-noise-model-to-hardware workflow but do not establish universal predictive accuracy, causal entanglement advantage, or quantum advantage over classical machine learning.

The primary contribution is methodological: longitudinal calibration collection, hardware-aware layout selection, preregistered simulation-to-hardware comparison, and auditable evidence preservation using consumer-grade, self-funded infrastructure.

1. Background and objective

The project began with a basic question: can I build and run a quantum neural network?

That question quickly became less interesting than a harder one. Hybrid quantum neural networks already exist. The more useful problem was whether their real-hardware behavior could be approximated using calibration telemetry collected independently over time.

That problem was directly relevant to the IRMB quantum-coordination research line. In Phase 7G Design 5, the real-IBM condition reversed against its classical controls. The experiment was complicated by several simultaneous factors, including council dropout caused by API limits, QPU decoherence, reduced parameter complexity, and elevated sampling temperature. The hardware measurements also showed no Bell violation, with |S| = 0.0120.

Design 5 could therefore not cleanly isolate whether its reversal represented a true negative result or a measurement corrupted by interacting implementation and hardware confounds.

QNN-P1 did not attempt to answer that causal question directly. It addressed a prerequisite:

Can longitudinal calibration data be turned into a useful instrument for modeling and planning real quantum execution?

2. Methods
Architecture

The study used a hybrid model containing a classical feature encoder, an eight-qubit variational quantum circuit, and a classical output head, implemented with PennyLane, PyTorch, Qiskit Aer, and Qiskit Runtime.

Three experimental arms formed a controlled ablation:

Model A: entangled hybrid VQC
Model B: classical control with an approximately parameter-matched MLP replacing the VQC
Model C: hybrid VQC with the entangling CZ operations removed

Model C retained the quantum circuit but removed inter-qubit entanglement, allowing the study to compare an entangled circuit against a product-state quantum control.

Task

The primary task used labels generated by a fixed random quantum circuit — chosen to move beyond heavily saturated demonstration datasets and reduce the risk that ceiling performance would obscure differences among the three arms. The labeling circuit, dataset generation, train/test split, and training seeds were fixed across the compared conditions.

Three-tier protocol

Tier 0 — ideal simulation. All three arms trained and evaluated under noiseless simulation across ten seeds, establishing whether the models learned the task and whether a clear performance difference existed before noise.

Tier 1 — archive-seeded noisy simulation. Qiskit Aer noise models were reconstructed from archived ibm_fez calibration snapshots, incorporating single- and two-qubit gate errors, thermal relaxation from T1/T2, readout error, and the physical qubits selected for hardware execution. The models deliberately did not represent every hardware effect; crosstalk, coherent error, and non-Markovian behavior remained outside the simulation and could therefore appear in the Tier 1-to-Tier 2 residual.

Tier 2 — IBM hardware. The trained Tier 1 checkpoints were evaluated on ibm_fez, evaluation-only, with no on-device training. A direct EstimatorV2 path submitted the complete test evaluation as a single Runtime job rather than one job per sample.

Qubit-chain selection

The hardware-aware layout procedure scored 950 candidate eight-qubit paths through the ibm_fez heavy-hex coupling graph using 20 archived daily calibration snapshots. The goal was not the path that looked best at one instant, but one with consistently favorable characteristics across the observed period.

The selected physical path was 125 → 124 → 123 → 136 → 143 → 142 → 141 → 140. It transpiled with zero inserted SWAP operations, circuit depth 21, and exactly 21 CZ gates.

Preregistration

The hardware protocol, endpoints, thresholds, analysis plan, known limitations, and checkpoint identities were committed before hardware results were unblinded. Protocol changes were preserved through a live deviations and amendments record.

3. Results
Tier 0 — no clear baseline separation

Across ten seeds per arm: Model A 0.739 ± 0.028, Model B 0.739 ± 0.053, Model C 0.745 ± 0.037. The means and standard deviations overlapped substantially; no clear noiseless performance separation was observed.

This mattered because it established a comparable baseline before archived noise and real-hardware execution. It does not prove that every later difference was caused exclusively by quantum noise, but it makes differences in noise response more interpretable under the matched protocol.

Physical-chain comparison

In one controlled Tier 1 comparison, model, seed, and calibration snapshot were held fixed while the represented physical region was changed:

Generic physical region, qubits 0–7: 0.7350
Archive-selected chain: 0.7850

Physical-chain choice produced a 5.0-percentage-point accuracy difference in this specific noisy-simulation comparison. This supports hardware-aware placement as an important experimental variable. It does not yet establish the same effect size across other seeds, tasks, snapshots, or matched hardware evaluations.

Calibration-date comparison

An 89% spread in chain-region composite error between the archive's best and worst calibration days produced only a 1.5-percentage-point, inverted accuracy difference (worst day 0.7850, best day 0.7700).

The interpretation is deliberately narrow: on this already-selected chain, where on the lattice the computation runs dominated performance, while when it ran did not measurably matter. The archive's demonstrated value in this study is layout selection, not favorable-day timing.

Both arms landed within half a point of the run-day simulation, with the residual in the same direction — hardware marginally above simulation, consistent with a slightly pessimistic first-order noise model. The A–C separation was 3.0 points in both simulation and hardware.

Preregistered endpoints, all passed:

P1: hardware A = 0.7850, inside the committed [0.72, 0.85] window and +4.7 points above the classical control.
P2: |A − C| = 0.030 ≤ 0.05, matching the simulation prediction of no separation beyond seed variance.
P3: sim-to-hardware gap ≈ 0 for both arms under run-day calibration.
4. What this study does not claim

The boundaries are part of the result. Hardware runs were single evaluations per arm, carrying roughly one point of measurement noise on 200 samples with no on-device error bars. One task family was used. Both hardware jobs ran in the same calibration window, leaving cross-day hardware variance unmeasured. The QNN demonstrated no quantum advantage — it tied the parameter-matched classical control, exactly as expected at eight qubits.

The defensible summary is narrow by design:

A self-collected calibration archive supported a first-order noise model and hardware-placement workflow that closely matched two preregistered hardware evaluations.

5. The deviations record

The project preserved a live deviations record because execution failures are part of the experimental method. During the hardware phase: initial cost was underestimated; early attempts were cancelled under an incorrect quota interpretation and flawed cost assumptions; the initial PennyLane path submitted approximately one Runtime job per circuit, making it impractical under the available account limits; the path was replaced with a direct single-job EstimatorV2 implementation; and the shot count was reduced from 4,096 to 1,024 after committed simulation reevaluations showed no compared accuracy change greater than 0.5 percentage points. Planned hardware replication was cancelled once the corrected cost model was established.

These changes were logged before the relevant hardware results were unblinded. The revised implementation added immediate job-ID persistence, recoverable job retrieval, a local dry-run path, preflight transpilation checks, and measured rather than assumed execution costs. The endpoint definitions and comparison anchors were retained throughout.

6. Position within the IRMB program

QNN-P1 belongs to a longer experimental sequence examining quantum-generated coordination signals and the conditions required to interpret them responsibly.

Design 3 — Bell Coordination. Tested whether real quantum-generated distributions influenced a multi-model LLM council differently from classical pseudorandom controls. The primary CHSH result was null, while supplementary analysis found quantum measurement distributions statistically distinguishable from sham-control distributions — separating quantum distributional influence from a demonstrated Bell-coordination effect.

Design 4 — Quantum Coordination. Evaluated 300 contested scientific claims across conditions including real IBM ibm_fez, classical pseudorandom generation, and Azure Quantinuum H2 emulation. It reproduced strong distributional divergence between IBM hardware and classical PRNG while finding no aggregate QPU performance advantage.

Design 5 — Quantum Causality. Introduced a matched-distribution classical control to separate quantum-process effects from distribution-shape effects. Results: C1 (IBM) F1 = 0.6136, C5 (matched control) 0.7767, C2 (PRNG) 0.7423. The primary endpoint reversed — C1 versus the average of its controls ΔF1 = −0.1459, and C1 versus C5 directly −0.1631. Critically, C5 versus C2 differed by only +0.0344, showing that classical performance was not explained solely by reproducing the shape of an earlier IBM measurement distribution — validating C5 as a genuine causal control.

The real-hardware condition was also affected by overlapping confounds. A seven-model meta-council estimated relative contributions from council dropout, QPU decoherence, parameter-complexity collapse, and elevated sampling temperature. Those percentages were model judgments, not experimentally isolated causal effects. The meta-council also produced a counterfactual estimate of F1 ≈ 0.7982 under hypothesized confound correction — a design hypothesis, not a measured result.

QNN-P1 does not close the Design 5 causal question. It begins to address one of the infrastructure problems Design 5 exposed: the need to observe, model, and document hardware conditions rather than treating the QPU as a static source. The QPU Drift Collector, developed in response to those concerns, supplied the longitudinal calibration data used here.

A confound identified in one study became the measurement target of the next.

7. Why this matters — and what comes next

The principal output of QNN-P1 is not a claim that an eight-qubit QNN beat classical machine learning. It is a pilot-tested apparatus: longitudinal calibration collection, raw-first append-only telemetry, archive-derived first-order noise models, hardware-aware chain scoring, zero-SWAP placement, preregistered simulation-to-hardware anchors, cost-aware single-job execution, and immutable run records with configuration fingerprints.

This apparatus provides a stronger starting point for two future directions.

Quantum error correction. QEC work requires realistic knowledge of the noise affecting the underlying hardware. The QNN-P1 archive and noise-modeling workflow can support exploratory questions such as: how would candidate repetition-code layouts behave under observed calibration conditions; which physical patches remain most stable across time; how sensitive is syndrome-decoder performance to the difference between static and time-varying noise; and which simplified noise assumptions fail when compared with hardware observations. These are future experiments, not results already established. The immediate value is that QEC planning can begin from an observed noise environment rather than an entirely idealized one.

Design 6. Intended to return to the causal question raised by Design 5 under stronger hardware-quality and execution controls — gating entanglement-modulated conditions on independently measured state quality before accepting them as valid quantum conditions. The drift archive and chain-selection workflow can support physical-qubit placement, pre-execution hardware qualification, calibration-state recording, cost and shot budgeting, post-run noise reconstruction, and a clear separation between measured outcomes and model-estimated counterfactuals.

QNN-P1 did not prove that calibration timing will improve Design 6 — its pilot timing comparison found no clear date effect after chain selection. The archive remains useful for monitoring, qualification, reconstruction, and identifying when hardware conditions materially depart from experimental assumptions.

QNN-P1 converted drift from an undocumented confound into an observable variable. QEC will test how errors can be detected and corrected under those observations. Design 6 can then test the earlier coordination hypothesis with stronger hardware qualification and cleaner causal controls.

8. Reproducibility

The repository is MIT-licensed and includes source code, a dependency lock, preregistration records, a reproduction guide, model and configuration fingerprints, hardware job IDs, calibration timestamps, and Tier 1 reevaluations.

Tier 0 can be run locally without an IBM Quantum account. Tier 1 requires the appropriate archived calibration snapshot. Hardware reproduction requires IBM Quantum access and will remain sensitive to backend availability, account limits, queue conditions, API changes, and the device's future calibration state. The QPU Drift Collector is publicly available and continues to operate as IRMB observational infrastructure.

QNN-P1 was independently designed, executed, and self-funded under Hudson Forge Technologies using consumer hardware, an open-access quantum account, and a Raspberry Pi 5 telemetry node.

Its strongest result is not that quantum defeated classical computation. It is that a small independent research operation built a complete path from raw hardware telemetry to preregistered simulation, real-QPU execution, failure logging, and preserved evidence — and showed that the path can produce testable, closely matched hardware predictions under clearly defined conditions.
